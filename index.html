Gig Economy Apps

Gig work refers to short-term, project-based, or on-demand jobs that individuals perform without being classified as traditional employees. These roles typically involve flexible hours, no long-term commitments, and compensation based on tasks or projects completed.

Gig workers are often self-employed or work through platforms that connect them with clients or customers. Think ride-share drivers, food delivery couriers, task-based app workers, and even creative professionals hired for one-off projects.

Gig economy apps connect freelance workers with short-term jobs in various fields, including ride-sharing, delivery, and freelancing. Popular examples include Uber and Lyft for transportation, DoorDash and Uber Eats for food delivery, and Fiverr and Upwork for professional services. Other apps like Instacart, Taskrabbit, and Rover focus on different services such as grocery shopping, handyman tasks, and pet care.

Trading

Trading, in a financial context, is the buying and selling of financial instruments—such as stocks, currencies, and commodities—with the goal of profiting from short-term price fluctuations. This differs from investing, which typically involves buying and holding assets for long-term wealth appreciation.

How Trading Works

Traders aim to profit by correctly predicting market price movements. If a trader expects an asset's price to rise, they "go long" (buy); if they expect it to fall, they "go short" (sell).

Supply and Demand: Prices are driven by supply and demand. More buyers than sellers increase prices, while more sellers than buyers decrease them.

Derivatives: Many traders, especially retail traders, use derivative products like Contracts for Difference (CFDs). These allow speculation on an asset's price without the trader ever owning the underlying asset.

Leverage: Derivatives often use leverage, allowing a trader to open a position much larger than their initial deposit. This magnifies both potential profits and losses, making risk management critical.

Key Markets and Assets

Traders can access a wide variety of global markets through online platforms.

Stocks/Shares: Buying and selling ownership stakes in publicly listed companies.

Forex (Foreign Exchange): The world's largest and most liquid market, involving the exchange of one currency for another (e.g., EUR/USD).

Commodities: Trading natural resources such as gold, oil, and agricultural products.

Indices: Trading on a collection of stocks that represent an entire market or sector, like the S&P 500 or FTSE 100.

Cryptocurrencies: Trading digital currencies such as Bitcoin and Ethereum.

Common Trading Styles

A trader's style depends on their time commitment, risk tolerance, and goals

Scalping (Micro Trading): A subset of day trading focusing on making many small profits from rapid trades within seconds or minutes.

Swing Trading: Positions are held for several days to a few weeks to profit from short to medium-term market trends.

Position Trading: A long-term approach where positions are held for months or years, similar to investing but focusing more on major price trends rather than fundamental ownership.

Getting Started

Educate Yourself: Learn about market dynamics, analysis (technical and fundamental), and risk management through educational resources.

Open a Brokerage Account: You need an account with a regulated broker that provides access to the markets you want to trade.

Create a Trading Plan: A crucial step that outlines your goals, strategy, and risk tolerance.

Practice: Many brokers offer demo accounts with virtual funds, allowing you to practice strategies risk-free before using real money

E-hailing Services

E-hailing services have transformed the transportation sector in South Africa. These services offer convenient, safe, and affordable travel choices, influencing urban mobility greatly. South Africa has seen an increase in e-hailing platforms catering to various needs and preferences, resulting in a diverse market.

Uber

Uber, which launched in South Africa in 2013, is the most well-known e-hailing service in the country. Operating in major cities such as Johannesburg, Cape Town, Durban, and Pretoria, Uber is popular and is now another word for ride-hailing in Mzansi. They also offer several options to cater to different needs

UberX – The most popular and affordable option.

UberXL – Suitable for larger groups or those needing more space

UberBLACK – A premium service offering high-end vehicles.

Uber

Bolt

Bolt, formerly known as Taxify, entered the South African market in 2016 and quickly became a serious competitor to Uber. Bolt is known for its low prices and good earnings for drivers, making it an attractive alternative. It operates in many cities and offers a service similar to Uber

Bolt Basics – Standard and cheap

Bolt XL – For larger groups or extra space

Bolt Premium – For the ultimate experience

Bolt’s competitive pricing and efficient service have made it a popular choice among South African commuters.

Bolt

DiDi

DiDi, a Chinese e-hailing giant, launched in South Africa in 2021. It has rapidly expanded, primarily targeting Johannesburg and Cape Town. DiDi offers competitive pricing and a strong focus on safety, including in-app emergency buttons and rigorous background checks for drivers. DiDi’s entry into the market has provided more options for consumers and increased competition.

DiDi

InDrive

InDrive, which operates on a unique negotiation model, allows passengers to propose their fares and drivers to accept, counter, or decline the offer. This model, introduced in South Africa in 2019, appeals to budget-conscious users and has gained popularity in cities like Johannesburg, Cape Town, and Durban

InDrive

Zebra Cabs

Zebra Cabs, while not as widely recognised as Uber or Bolt, offers a reliable e-hailing service primarily in Johannesburg and Pretoria. Zebra Cabs distinguishes itself by integrating traditional metered taxis with modern app-based booking, providing a hybrid solution that appeals to a broad range of users.

Shesha

Launched on 1 May 2024, Shesha uses a cashless system through a secure wallet system that will provide safety for both riders and drivers. An emergency ride for clients with a history of 10 or more trips is also available and ensures a safety net during unforeseen circumstances

Shesha

BozaRide

BozaRide launched in three major metropolitan municipalities in Gauteng on 29 March 2024. BozaRide uses a facial biometric verification system to avoid registration of multiple accounts by the same users and unauthorised uses of the app. The app has a mandatory one-time pin functionality for all trips, a zone risk feature (to alert drivers when driving in high-risk areas), and another functionality to disclose the number of riders upon request.
